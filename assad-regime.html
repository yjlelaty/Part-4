The Assad Regime

The Assad regime has played a significant role in shaping modern Syria. Initially coming to power in 1971, Hafez al-Assad established a tightly controlled authoritarian state. After his death, his son Bashar al-Assad took over, continuing many of the same policies that emphasized centralized power, suppression of dissent, and cultivating a strong security apparatus. These practices set the stage for widespread discontent that ultimately contributed to the Syrian revolution.

The regime's rule has been marked by numerous human rights violations, lack of political freedom, and severe economic disparities. Over time, the public's frustration grew, eventually igniting the revolution during the broader Arab Spring movement. Understanding the Assad family's grip on power is crucial to comprehending the roots of Syria's ongoing crisis and the hardships faced by millions under their rule.
